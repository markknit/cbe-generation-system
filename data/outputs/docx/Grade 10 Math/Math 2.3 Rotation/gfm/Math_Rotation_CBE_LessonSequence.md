# Mathematics Grade 10: Rotation
## CBE Phenomenon-Driven Lesson Sequence — Sub-Strand 2.3 (6 Lessons)

## Sub-Strand Overview

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Grade Level** | Grade 10 |
| **Subject** | Mathematics |
| **Strand** | Strand 2.0: Measurement and Geometry |
| **Sub-Strand** | Sub-Strand 2.3: Rotation |
| **Total Duration** | 6 lessons × 4 periods × 40 minutes = 960 minutes total |
| **Key Inquiry Question** | How does rotation — turning around a fixed point — create the motion and patterns that power machines, keep time, and generate energy in Kenya and beyond? |
| **Driving Question** | What mathematical rules describe how a point (or shape) moves when it rotates around a fixed centre — and how do engineers use these rules to design turbines, gears, and clocks? |

### Learning Outcomes
By the end of the sub-strand, the learner should be able to:  
a) identify the centre, angle, and direction of rotation  
b) rotate a shape about the origin through given angles  
c) rotate a shape about any given point  
d) determine the centre and angle of rotation given an object and its image  
e) state the properties of rotation  
f) apply rotation in real-life situations

### Anchoring Phenomenon
The Ngong Hills Wind Farm (Lake Turkana Wind Power) has 365 wind turbines, each with 3 blades rotating about a central hub. A short time-lapse video shows one turbine blade tracing a full circle as it rotates 360°. Students observe: the blade tip travels in a circle; the hub (centre) stays fixed; different blades reach the same positions one after another.  
Secondary: the second hand of a clock on the class wall makes exactly one rotation per minute. Anchor question: "What mathematical rules describe the journey of a point rotating around a fixed centre?"

### Storyline Thread
L1: Observe wind turbine rotation → identify centre, angle, direction → initial model  
L2: Rotation about the origin — derive coordinate rules for 90°, 180°, 270°  
L3: Rotation about any point — generalise using translation + origin rotation  
L4: Properties of rotation — prove distances and angles are preserved  
L5: Finding centre and angle of rotation — working backwards from object + image  
L6: Real-world design challenge — turbine blade path, clock hands, gear ratios

## Lesson 1: What Is Rotation? Centre, Angle, and Direction

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students observe and describe rotation in Kenyan real-world contexts, identifying the three defining elements: centre of rotation, angle of rotation, and direction.

**Knowledge:**  
• Define rotation as a transformation that turns a shape around a fixed point  
• Identify the centre of rotation, angle of rotation, and direction (clockwise/anticlockwise)  
• Distinguish rotation from reflection and translation

**Skills:**  
• Identify the three rotation parameters in physical and photographic examples  
• Estimate angles of rotation from visual inspection  
• Use tracing paper to perform simple rotations

**Attitudes:**  
• Curiosity about the mathematics behind everyday rotating machines  
• Appreciation of Kenya's renewable energy investment (Ngong Hills Wind Farm)

**Purpose in Storyline:** This lesson anchors the driving question in the wind turbine phenomenon. Students build intuitive understanding of the three rotation parameters before formal rules are introduced.

**Safety Notes:** Scissors for cutting tracing paper — use safety scissors.

### B. Lesson Overview

The lesson opens with a 90-second time-lapse video of the Ngong Hills Wind Farm at night (blades illuminated) and a close-up of a clock second hand. Students discuss: "What is the same about these two motions? What is different?" The class identifies: both have a fixed centre, both move through angles, both have a direction of spin.  
  
Students then use tracing paper over printed diagrams to physically rotate shapes by given angles. The DQB is opened with questions about rotation rules, coordinate effects, and applications. Each student draws their initial model: "What is rotation and how does it work?"

## Lesson 2: Rotation About the Origin — Coordinate Rules

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students derive and apply coordinate rules for rotation about the origin through 90°, 180°, and 270°.

**Knowledge:**  
• State coordinate rules: 90° ACW → (x,y) → (−y,x); 180° → (−x,−y); 270° ACW → (y,−x)  
• Explain why 90° CW gives the same image as 270° ACW  
• State that 360° rotation maps every point to itself

**Skills:**  
• Apply coordinate rules to rotate points and polygons about the origin  
• Plot images on the Cartesian plane and verify using tracing paper  
• Identify the rotation from object and image coordinates

**Attitudes:**  
• Appreciation that algebraic rules make geometric transformations precise and fast  
• Confidence with coordinate manipulation

**Purpose in Storyline:** The coordinate rules are the algebraic heart of rotation. Students move from the physical tracing-paper model to a precise mathematical rule that can be applied to any point without drawing.

**Safety Notes:** No specific safety notes.

### B. Lesson Overview

Students plot triangle A(1,2), B(3,1), C(2,4) on squared paper and rotate it 90° ACW about the origin using tracing paper. They record the image coordinates and look for the pattern. The rule (x,y)→(−y,x) is derived from the data. They then derive rules for 180° and 270° ACW by the same method.  
  
GeoGebra is used to explore fractional and larger angles (e.g., 45°, 135°, 450°) as an extension. The turbine context is revisited: if a blade tip is at coordinates (0,5) — where is it after one-third turn (120°)?

## Lesson 3: Rotation About Any Point

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students extend rotation to any centre point — not just the origin — using translation + origin rotation + translation back.

**Knowledge:**  
• Explain the three-step method: translate so centre becomes origin, rotate, translate back  
• Apply the method to rotate a shape about any point  
• Verify that the distance from each image vertex to the centre equals the distance from the object vertex

**Skills:**  
• Rotate a triangle about a centre point that is not the origin  
• Apply the three-step coordinate method accurately  
• Use tracing paper to verify results

**Attitudes:**  
• Persistence through multi-step calculations  
• Appreciation that complex problems can be broken into simpler steps

**Purpose in Storyline:** Generalising from the origin to any centre is a key conceptual step. Students see that the origin rule is a special case of a general method.

**Safety Notes:** No specific safety notes.

### B. Lesson Overview

Teacher introduces the challenge: "Rotate triangle A(4,5), B(6,3), C(5,7) by 90° ACW about centre P(2,1)." Students attempt it directly — most will struggle or make errors. The teacher then reveals the three-step method: (1) translate so P becomes the origin (subtract P from all coordinates), (2) apply the 90° ACW origin rule, (3) translate back (add P to all coordinates).  
  
Students practise with two further examples and verify with tracing paper. Context: the centre of a gear wheel is at (3,2) — rotate a tooth tip through 60°.

### D. Teacher Reflection

After teaching this lesson, reflect on:  
  
1. Did the structured failure (applying origin rule to non-origin centre) effectively motivate the three-step method?  
  
2. Step 1 (subtracting centre) and Step 3 (adding centre back): did students understand WHY these steps work?  
  
3. Most common error: subtracting the centre in Step 1 but forgetting to add it back in Step 3.  
  
4. Could students generalise the method to a formula with centre (a,b)? Or did they need to work through concrete examples only?  
  
5. Partner checking: did students correctly identify and fix each other's errors?  
  
6. Did the gear wheel context (centre at (3,2)) add relevance, or was it confusing?

## Lesson 4: Properties of Rotation

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students investigate and prove the key properties of rotation: distances and angles are preserved, the centre is equidistant from object and image vertices.

**Knowledge:**  
• State that rotation preserves lengths (object and image are congruent)  
• State that rotation preserves angles (angle between any two lines is preserved)  
• State that the centre of rotation is equidistant from any object vertex and its corresponding image vertex  
• Distinguish rotation from dilation (rotation preserves size; dilation does not)

**Skills:**  
• Measure distances from centre to object and image vertices  
• Verify angle preservation using a protractor  
• Use properties to identify whether a transformation is a rotation

**Attitudes:**  
• Mathematical rigour: proving properties rather than assuming them  
• Appreciation that preserving distances and angles is what makes rotation useful in engineering

**Purpose in Storyline:** Properties confirm that rotation is a rigid transformation (isometry). This connects rotation to congruence (Lesson 2.2) and prepares for the centre-finding method (Lesson 5).

**Safety Notes:** Compass points are sharp — use with care.

### B. Lesson Overview

Students begin with a congruence question: "A turbine blade rotates 120°. Is the blade's shape and size preserved, or does rotation distort it?" The answer is yes — preserved. This motivates a formal investigation.  
  
Groups measure distances from centre of rotation to object vertices (OA, OB, OC) and to image vertices (OA', OB', OC'). They compare. They also measure object side lengths and image side lengths. The class derives: rotation is an isometry (rigid transformation). Finally, students learn to use the equidistance property to IDENTIFY the centre — by drawing circles around corresponding vertex pairs.

### C. Lesson Implementation Framework

| Phase | Learner Experience | Resource | Teacher Actions | Sensemaking Strategy | Assessment Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Predict Phase** | "After rotating triangle ABC 90° ACW about origin O, is triangle A'B'C' congruent to ABC? Which of the four congruence tests (from Lesson 2.2) would prove it? Predict before measuring." | MATERIAL: Previously drawn rotation diagrams from Lesson 2 or 3 | "Predict: are the side lengths the same? The angles? What do you expect from a 'turning' transformation?" | Connecting rotation to congruence (prior unit) creates cross-topic coherence. | Do students predict congruence correctly? Do they suggest SSS as the test? |
| **Observe Phase** | Measurement investigation: 1. On a rotation diagram (triangle rotated about O), measure: OA, OA', OB, OB', OC, OC'. 2. Compare and tabulate. 3. Measure side lengths: AB, A'B', BC, B'C'. 4. Measure angles: ∠ABC and ∠A'B'C'. 5. Conclusion: what is preserved? | MATERIAL: Protractors, rulers, rotation diagrams (printed, accurately constructed) DIGITAL: GeoGebra to dynamically show that properties hold for ANY angle | "Measure to the nearest millimetre. Do OA and OA' match exactly?" "What word describes a transformation that preserves all lengths and angles?" | Empirical verification of properties. Measurement confirms what coordinate algebra implies. | Results: OA = OA', OB = OB', OC = OC'; side lengths preserved; angles preserved. Deviations larger than 0.5 mm indicate construction errors. |
| **Explain Phase** | Formally state three properties: 1. Distances preserved: \|OP\| = \|OP'\| for every point P 2. Lengths preserved: \|AB\| = \|A'B'\| (rotation is an isometry) 3. Angles preserved: ∠ABC = ∠A'B'C' Isometry: a transformation that preserves all distances. (Rotation, reflection, and translation are all isometries.) Application: use property 1 to identify the centre — the centre is the point equidistant from P and P' for ALL corresponding pairs. | MATERIAL: Whiteboard; student measurement tables | "The centre is the UNIQUE point equidistant from each object-image pair. How would you find it geometrically? (Hint: circles or perpendicular bisectors.)" | Formal statement of properties from empirical findings. Centre-finding hint previews Lesson 5. | "True or false: rotation changes the size of a shape." (False — rotation is an isometry.) "True or false: rotation changes the orientation of a shape." (True — orientation is reversed for reflection, but for rotation it is preserved; clarify this common confusion.) |
| **Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation** | Update DQB. Move "properties" questions to ANSWERED. Focus on remaining question: "How do I find the centre and angle if I only have object and image?" | MATERIAL: DQB board | "That is exactly what Lesson 5 teaches. The equidistance property is the key." | DQB creates direct anticipation for Lesson 5. | Students should now be comfortable with isometry and ready for centre-finding. |
| **Model Building Phase** | Update Rotation Model: add the three properties. Label the model diagram with: distance from centre to object vertex = distance from centre to image vertex. | MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 4 revision) | "Your model should now prove that rotation preserves congruence — using SSS." | Model integrates isometry concept with congruence knowledge from prior unit. | Do models correctly state all three properties? Do they connect to congruence tests? |

### D. Teacher Reflection

After teaching this lesson, reflect on:  
  
1. Did students connect rotation properties to congruence (Sub-Strand 2.2) effectively? Which congruence test did they apply?  
  
2. Common confusion: "rotation changes orientation." In fact, rotation preserves orientation (unlike reflection). Did this arise? How did you clarify?  
  
3. Were measurement results accurate enough to see that OA = OA'? What precision issues arose?  
  
4. GeoGebra: did the dynamic demonstration of properties help students trust the measurements?

6. Centre-finding hint: did students see how the equidistance property leads to a perpendicular bisector method?

## Lesson 5: Finding the Centre and Angle of Rotation

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students determine the centre and angle of rotation given an object and its image, completing the full inverse problem of rotation.

**Knowledge:**  
• State that the centre of rotation lies on the perpendicular bisector of any segment PP'  
• Use two perpendicular bisectors to find the centre of rotation by intersection  
• Calculate the angle of rotation using the angle ∠POP'

**Skills:**  
• Construct perpendicular bisectors of at least two PP' segments  
• Find their intersection to locate the centre  
• Use a protractor to measure ∠POP' to find the angle of rotation

**Attitudes:**  
• Persistence with multi-step constructions  
• Appreciation that inverse problems are as important as direct problems

**Purpose in Storyline:** The inverse problem — finding centre and angle — completes the rotation model. Students can now move in both directions: apply a rotation OR identify one from evidence.

**Safety Notes:** Compass use — sharp points, handle carefully.

### B. Lesson Overview

The lesson opens with an engineering challenge: "A gear tooth has moved from position A to position A'. Find the centre of the gear wheel (the centre of rotation) and the angle it has turned."  
  
Students recall the Lesson 4 property: the centre is equidistant from P and P'. This means it lies on the perpendicular bisector of PP'. Construct two perpendicular bisectors — their intersection IS the centre. Then measure ∠POP' for the angle. Students apply the method to two challenge problems.

## Lesson 6: Real-World Design Challenge — Turbines, Gears, and Clocks

**Duration:** 4 periods / 160 minutes  
**Key Inquiry:** How does rotation create the motion and patterns that power machines and keep time?

### A. Specific Learning Outcomes

**Purpose:** Students apply all rotation knowledge in a real-world engineering design challenge and write their final explanation.

**Knowledge:**  
• Connect all five lessons into a unified model of rotation  
• Apply rotation rules to design a clock or turbine blade diagram  
• Explain how rotation is used in real Kenyan engineering contexts

**Skills:**  
• Design a symmetric 3-blade turbine pattern using 120° rotations about the hub  
• Calculate positions of all three blades at any time step  
• Write a complete mathematical explanation answering the driving question

**Attitudes:**  
• Pride in applying mathematics to real engineering problems  
• Appreciation of Kenya's renewable energy sector  
• Confidence to explain complex mathematical ideas in writing

**Purpose in Storyline:** Unit closure. The design challenge applies all six lessons. The final explanation closes the driving question. Comparing Lesson 1 and Lesson 6 models shows full learning arc.

**Safety Notes:** No specific safety notes.

### B. Lesson Overview

The lesson presents two design challenges (students choose one):  
  
CHALLENGE A (Turbine Design): Design a 3-blade wind turbine diagram. Blade 1 starts at position B₁(0,4). Find positions of all three blades (120° apart). Then calculate all blade positions after the turbine rotates 30°. Label all coordinates and state the rotation rules used.  
  
CHALLENGE B (Clock Design): Design a clock face. Mark 12 hour positions as 30° rotation steps. Find the coordinates of the hour hand tip (length 3 units) at 12:00, 3:00, 6:00, 9:00, and 10:00. Verify using the appropriate rotation rules.  
  
Both challenges require: coordinate calculations, rotation rules, and a written explanation. Lesson closes with model comparison and Final Explanation writing.
