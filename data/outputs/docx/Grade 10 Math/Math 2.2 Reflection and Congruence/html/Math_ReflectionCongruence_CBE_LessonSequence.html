LESSON 1: Lines of Symmetry — What Makes a Shape Balanced?
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents discover that many shapes and real objects have lines of symmetry, and begin building a model of what "reflection" means mathematically.
Knowledge• Define a line of symmetry as a line that divides a shape into two congruent halves
• Identify the number of lines of symmetry in common plane figures (triangle, square, rectangle, circle, regular hexagon)
• Distinguish between shapes with and without symmetry
Skills• Fold physical shapes to locate lines of symmetry
• Draw lines of symmetry accurately using a ruler
• Categorise Kenyan objects by number of lines of symmetry
Attitudes• Appreciation of mathematical order in natural and man-made Kenyan objects
• Curiosity about why some shapes have more lines of symmetry than others
Purpose in StorylineThis lesson anchors the driving question in visible Kenyan contexts. Students move from informal notions of "balanced" to the precise mathematical idea of a line of symmetry. The DQB is opened; initial models of reflection are drawn.
Safety NotesUse safety scissors only. Handle mirrors carefully — no glass mirrors; use polished metal or plastic mirrors.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Students begin with a gallery walk: eight photographs pinned around the room — KICC dome, Lake Nakuru flamingos, kanga textile, a Kenyan flag, a butterfly, a starfish, the letter "A", and a freehand blob. Groups discuss: "Which of these has balance — and where exactly is the balance line?"

After sharing, the teacher introduces the term "line of symmetry" and students test their claims by folding cut-out shapes. The DQB is opened and students post their first questions. The lesson closes with each student drawing their initial model: "What is reflection?"

What did I learn?A line of symmetry divides a shape into two congruent mirror-image halves. Regular polygons have the same number of lines of symmetry as sides. Not all shapes are symmetric — a scalene triangle has no lines of symmetry.
How does this explain the phenomenon?The KICC dome and flamingo flock both show bilateral symmetry — one half mirrors the other exactly. But we do not yet know the MATHEMATICAL RULE that makes this work. That is what Lessons 2–5 will reveal.
LESSON 2: Properties of Reflection — Discovering the Mirror Rules
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents investigate and state the properties of reflection: equal distance from mirror line, perpendicular to mirror line, same size and shape, reversed orientation.
Knowledge• State that object and image are equidistant from the mirror line
• State that the line joining any point to its image is perpendicular to the mirror line
• State that object and image are congruent (same size and shape)
• Explain that reflection reverses orientation (left-right flip)
Skills• Use a plane mirror to locate the image of a point and measure distances
• Verify the perpendicularity property using a set square
• Record and tabulate experimental results
Attitudes• Scientific curiosity: testing claims rather than assuming
• Precision: measuring distances accurately
Purpose in StorylineStudents shift from observing symmetry to discovering the mathematical rules that govern reflection. The four properties become the core content of the unit — all subsequent lessons apply these rules.
Safety NotesUse plastic/polished-metal mirrors only. Ensure mirror edges are smooth.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Students work in pairs with a plastic mirror and a printed worksheet showing points and shapes. They place the mirror on a drawn line and observe where the image appears. They measure distances from points to the mirror line and from images to the mirror line — discovering that these are always equal.

A second investigation tests perpendicularity: students connect each point to its image and check the angle with the mirror line. The class consolidates the four properties. DQB questions about "rules" are moved to ANSWERED. Models are updated.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase Pose: "If I place a point 3 cm from the mirror line — where will its image be? Predict and justify." Groups write predictions on mini-whiteboards before using mirrors. MATERIAL: Mini-whiteboards or A4 paper, markers "Do not use the mirror yet. Commit to a prediction first."
"3 cm from the line — same side as the object? Other side? How far?"		 Prediction before observation creates cognitive investment in the outcome. Listen for the "equal distance" idea — who already has this intuition?
Observe Phase Investigation with mirror and worksheet:
• Step 1: Mark point P at 3 cm from mirror line L. Place mirror on L. Mark image P'.
• Step 2: Measure PP' distance. Measure distance from P to L and from P' to L.
• Step 3: Repeat for P at 1 cm, 5 cm, 7 cm. Record in table.
• Step 4: Draw line PP'. Measure the angle it makes with L.		 MATERIAL: Plastic mirrors, rulers, set squares, printed worksheets, pencils
DIGITAL: GeoGebra Reflection tool (for verification)		 "Measure carefully — to the nearest millimetre."
"What is the angle between PP' and L in every case? What word describes this?"		 Data collection from physical experiment. Repeated measurement across different distances builds inductive reasoning. Check tables: are students measuring perpendicular distances from point to line (not diagonal)? Common error: measuring along the page rather than perpendicularly.
Explain Phase Groups present findings. Class derives and writes the four properties of reflection:
1. Object distance = Image distance from mirror line
2. PP' is perpendicular to the mirror line
3. Object and image are congruent (same size and shape)
4. Image is a lateral (left-right) inversion of the object		 MATERIAL: Whiteboard; student-made tables "State each property as a rule using the word 'always'."
"Which of these properties explains why a mirror writing looks reversed?"		 Inductive reasoning: from specific measurements to general rules. Students own the properties because they discovered them. Quick-check: "A point is 4.5 cm from the mirror line. How far is its image from the mirror line? How far is the image from the original point?"
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Review DQB from Lesson 1. Move "rules" questions to ANSWERED. Add new questions: "How do I reflect a SHAPE, not just a point?" "What if the mirror line is diagonal?" "Can the mirror line be y = x?" MATERIAL: DQB board, coloured sticky notes (new colour for Lesson 2 additions) "Which questions from Lesson 1 can we now answer? Bring them to the front." DQB curation shows students their own learning progression. New questions preview Lessons 3–5 focus areas.
Model Building Phase Update Reflection Model: add the four properties discovered today. Draw a labelled diagram showing a point P, its image P', the mirror line L, and the two equal distances. MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 2 revision) "Your model must now include a diagram AND a written rule for each property."
"Compare your Lesson 1 and Lesson 2 models — what changed?"		 Model revision makes learning visible and prepares students for Lessons 3–4. Do models correctly show perpendicularity and equal distances?
D. TEACHER REFLECTION
After teaching this lesson, reflect on:

1. Did the prediction-before-observation structure improve engagement? Did students genuinely commit to predictions?

2. Common measurement error: were students measuring diagonal distances instead of perpendicular? How did you address it?

3. Which of the four properties caused most confusion? (Often: perpendicularity and lateral inversion.)

4. Did GeoGebra help or distract? Would you use it earlier or later in the lesson?

5. How strong were student model revisions? Did they add all four properties or only the equal-distance rule?

6. Are DQB questions now pointing toward Lessons 3–5 topics (shape reflection, Cartesian plane)?
E. SUMMARY TABLE PROMPT (pre-filled example)
What did I observe?Measured distances from points to mirror line and from their images to mirror line — always equal. Drew PP' lines and measured the angle with the mirror line — always 90°. Confirmed that object and image are same size and shape (congruent) but laterally inverted.
What did I learn?Four properties of reflection: (1) equal distance from mirror line, (2) PP' perpendicular to mirror line, (3) object ≅ image (congruent), (4) image is laterally inverted. These four rules ALWAYS hold — for any point, any mirror line.
How does this explain the phenomenon?The KICC dome is symmetric because every point on the left side is the mirror image of the corresponding point on the right — same distance from the central axis, perpendicular connection. The mathematical rules we discovered today are exactly what the architects used.
LESSON 3: Reflection on Plane Figures — Drawing the Image
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents apply the four reflection properties to draw the image of a geometric shape given an object and a mirror line.
Knowledge• State the step-by-step procedure for reflecting a shape over a given mirror line
• Identify corresponding vertices in object and image
• Verify the image using the perpendicularity and equal-distance properties
Skills• Draw the reflection of a triangle, quadrilateral, and irregular shape over a given mirror line
• Reflect shapes over horizontal, vertical, and diagonal mirror lines
• Use a ruler and set square accurately
Attitudes• Precision and patience in geometric construction
• Confidence to attempt reflections over non-horizontal/vertical lines
Purpose in StorylineStudents transition from knowing the rules to applying them constructively. This lesson is procedural and skill-building — the foundation for Lesson 4 (Cartesian plane).
Safety NotesCompass points are sharp — use with care and store with cover.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Teacher models the step-by-step construction: (1) identify each vertex, (2) draw a perpendicular from each vertex to the mirror line, (3) extend the perpendicular an equal distance beyond the mirror line, (4) mark the image vertex, (5) connect image vertices in order.

Students then practise in three rounds: mirror line is horizontal → vertical → diagonal at 45°. A challenge extension: reflect a Kanga-inspired pattern tile over two lines to create a 2×2 symmetric tile.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase Shown a triangle ABC and a mirror line L. "Before drawing — where will the image be? Sketch your prediction." Focus on vertex A (closest to L), B (farthest), C (on L). MATERIAL: Printed worksheet with triangle and mirror line; pencils "Pay attention to vertex C — it sits ON the mirror line. What happens to a point that is on the line?" Predicting specific vertex positions activates the equal-distance property. Check: do students correctly predict that C maps to itself (it is on the mirror line)?
Observe Phase Teacher-led demonstration of construction steps on the board. Students follow along on their own worksheet simultaneously. After teacher demonstration, students attempt a second triangle independently. MATERIAL: Rulers, set squares, compasses (optional for marking equal distances)
VISUAL: Step-by-step construction poster on board		 "I draw a perpendicular from A to L — I need my set square for this, not just a ruler."
"I measure 2.5 cm from A to L. I count 2.5 cm beyond L on the other side. That is A'."		 Worked example followed immediately by independent practice (I Do → We Do → You Do). Circulate during independent practice. Common errors: not extending perpendicular far enough, measuring along a non-perpendicular path.
Explain Phase Three practice rounds:
• Round 1: Reflect quadrilateral PQRS over a horizontal mirror line
• Round 2: Reflect triangle DEF over a vertical mirror line
• Round 3: Reflect triangle GHI over a diagonal mirror line (45°)
Pairs check each other's work using the four properties.		 MATERIAL: Printed practice sheets (3 rounds), rulers, set squares
EXTENSION: Kanga tile design challenge (optional)		 "Use the four properties to VERIFY your answer — do not just assume your drawing is right."
"Round 3 is the hardest. What changes when the mirror line is diagonal?"		 Graduated difficulty: horizontal → vertical → diagonal. Peer verification reinforces the four properties as checking tools. Collect Round 3 drawings — accuracy here indicates readiness for Lesson 4.
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Update DQB. New questions likely: "How do we do this on a coordinate grid?" "Is there a formula?" "What if the shape overlaps the mirror line?" MATERIAL: DQB board "Lesson 4 will answer the coordinate grid questions. Let us add them now." DQB maintains forward momentum — each lesson creates appetite for the next. New questions confirm students are ready for Cartesian plane work.
Model Building Phase Update Reflection Model: add a labelled example of a reflected shape with all five construction steps annotated. MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 3 revision) "Your model should now show the PROCEDURE, not just the properties." Procedural knowledge added to the model — connects rules to action. Do models show all five steps in correct order?
D. TEACHER REFLECTION
After teaching this lesson, reflect on:

1. Did students predict vertex C correctly (maps to itself)? If not, how did you clarify?

2. How accurate were Round 3 (diagonal mirror line) constructions? What is the most common error?

3. Was the I Do → We Do → You Do sequence effective, or did students need more scaffolding?

4. Did the Kanga challenge extension engage higher-ability students without losing others?

5. Peer verification: did students use the four properties to check, or did they just look visually?

6. What preparation do students still need before the Cartesian plane lesson?
E. SUMMARY TABLE PROMPT (pre-filled example)
What did I observe?Practised five-step construction: draw perpendicular from vertex → measure distance to mirror line → extend equal distance beyond → mark image vertex → connect vertices. Reflected shapes over horizontal, vertical, and diagonal mirror lines.
What did I learn?The five-step procedure applies to any mirror line orientation. A point ON the mirror line maps to itself. The image is always congruent to the object — same size, reversed orientation.
How does this explain the phenomenon?The flamingo mirror image in Lake Nakuru water is created by the same five steps we used today — every point on the flamingo reflects equally to the water surface (the mirror line). We can now construct any reflected image precisely.
LESSON 4: Reflection on the Cartesian Plane
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents extend reflection to the Cartesian plane, applying coordinate rules for reflection over the x-axis, y-axis, the line y = x, and the line y = -x.
Knowledge• State coordinate rules: reflection over x-axis (x,y) → (x,−y); over y-axis → (−x,y); over y = x → (y,x); over y = −x → (−y,−x)
• Identify which axis or line is the mirror line from a plotted object and image
Skills• Plot points and their images on the Cartesian plane
• Apply coordinate rules to reflect polygons
• Verify reflections using the four properties
Attitudes• Appreciation that algebraic rules make geometric construction faster and more precise
• Confidence with coordinate geometry
Purpose in StorylineThe lesson bridges the geometric procedure of Lesson 3 with algebraic rules. Students see that the Cartesian plane gives us shortcuts — the coordinate rules replace the five-step construction.
Safety NotesNo specific safety notes.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Students first plot a triangle on squared paper and reflect it over the x-axis using the Lesson 3 construction. They then record the coordinates of each vertex and its image, looking for the pattern. The four coordinate rules are derived from the data. Students then practise applying the rules directly — no construction needed, just the algebraic rule.

GeoGebra is used for self-checking and for exploring y = x and y = -x reflections.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase Given triangle A(2,3), B(5,1), C(4,5). "If you reflect over the x-axis, predict the coordinates of A', B', C'." No grid provided — pure coordinate reasoning. MATERIAL: Blank paper for predictions, pencils "What does the x-axis do to the y-coordinate of a point above it?"
"The x-axis is the mirror line. A is 3 units above it. Where will A' be?"		 Numerical prediction connects the equal-distance property to coordinate arithmetic. Do students predict (2,−3)? If not, they are not yet applying the equal-distance rule numerically.
Observe Phase On squared paper: plot A(2,3), B(5,1), C(4,5). Use Lesson 3 construction to reflect over x-axis. Record: A(2,3)→A'(_,_), B(5,1)→B'(_,_), C(4,5)→C'(_,_). Repeat for reflection over y-axis. Record coordinates. Look for the pattern: what changes? What stays the same? MATERIAL: Squared paper, rulers, pencils
DIGITAL: GeoGebra Cartesian Reflection (self-check after manual work)		 "Fill in the table first — then look for the pattern."
"Don't tell me the rule yet. What do you NOTICE?"		 Pattern recognition from tabulated data. Students derive the algebraic rule inductively. Table completion: are coordinates correct to verify pattern? Check A'(2,−3), B'(5,−1), C'(4,−5).
Explain Phase Class derives four rules from the table:
• Over x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y) — y changes sign
• Over y-axis: (x, y) → (−x, y) — x changes sign
• Over y = x: (x, y) → (y, x) — coordinates swap
• Over y = −x: (x, y) → (−y, −x) — swap then negate both
Students practise 6 coordinate-rule exercises.		 MATERIAL: Exercise worksheet with 6 problems
DIGITAL: GeoGebra for y = x and y = −x exploration		 "The rule replaces the five-step construction. But you should understand WHY the rule works, not just what it is."
"Can you explain the y = x rule using the equal-distance property?"		 Algebraic generalisation. Students see that coordinate rules are the equal-distance property expressed algebraically. Worksheet completion: mark problems 5 and 6 (y = x and y = −x) — these are the hardest.
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Update DQB. New possible questions: "How do I find the mirror line equation from object + image?" "Can the mirror line be any line, like y = 2x?" Add these for Lesson 5. MATERIAL: DQB board "Lesson 5 answers the 'find the mirror line' questions." DQB continues to drive curiosity forward. New questions confirm conceptual readiness for Lesson 5.
Model Building Phase Update Reflection Model: add the four coordinate rules as a table. Show a worked example using coordinates. MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 4 revision) "Your model is now algebraic AND geometric. That is powerful." Model integrates geometric and algebraic representations. Do models correctly state all four coordinate rules?
D. TEACHER REFLECTION
After teaching this lesson, reflect on:

1. Did students make the connection between equal-distance property and coordinate arithmetic (e.g., y changes sign for x-axis reflection)?

2. The y = x rule (coordinates swap) is counterintuitive — how did students react? What explanation worked best?

3. Did GeoGebra accelerate understanding or become a distraction from working through the derivation manually?

4. Worksheet accuracy on y = −x problems — this is the hardest transformation. What errors did you see?

5. Did students connect this lesson back to the KICC phenomenon — e.g., "the central axis of KICC is the y-axis"?

6. How confident did students seem about Lesson 5 (finding the mirror line)? What do you need to pre-teach?
E. SUMMARY TABLE PROMPT (pre-filled example)
What did I observe?Plotted triangles on Cartesian plane and reflected over x-axis and y-axis using the Lesson 3 construction. Tabulated coordinates of object and image points. Identified patterns → derived four coordinate rules. Used GeoGebra to explore y = x and y = −x reflections.
What did I learn?Four coordinate reflection rules: over x-axis → (x,y)→(x,−y); over y-axis → (−x,y); over y=x → (y,x); over y=−x → (−y,−x). These rules ARE the equal-distance property expressed algebraically.
How does this explain the phenomenon?The Kanga textile pattern uses exactly these rules — the designer reflects a tile over the x-axis and y-axis to create a four-fold symmetric pattern. We can now describe any reflection in coordinates.
LESSON 5: Finding the Mirror Line — Working Backwards
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents determine the equation of the mirror line given an object and its image, completing the full cycle of reflection understanding.
Knowledge• State that the mirror line is the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining any point to its image
• Determine the equation of the mirror line given pairs of corresponding points
Skills• Construct the perpendicular bisector of PP' to find the mirror line
• Use midpoint and gradient methods to find the mirror line equation
• Verify the equation using at least two point pairs
Attitudes• Persistence with multi-step problems
• Appreciation that working backwards is a powerful problem-solving strategy
Purpose in StorylineThe inverse problem — finding the mirror line — completes the model. Students can now both construct a reflection AND identify the rule from a given image. This makes the model fully two-directional.
Safety NotesNo specific safety notes.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
The lesson opens with an "unknown mirror" challenge: students are shown an object and image but NOT the mirror line. They must find it. This reverses all previous work.

Students recall that PP' is always perpendicular to the mirror line and that the mirror line bisects PP'. They construct the perpendicular bisector of at least two PP' segments — these bisectors coincide to reveal the mirror line. The equation is then found using gradient and intercept.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase Shown: point P(1,2) and its image P'(5,4). "Where is the mirror line? Predict its equation." No construction tools yet — just reasoning from the properties. MATERIAL: Squared paper, pencils "What do you know about the relationship between PP' and the mirror line?"
"If you knew the midpoint of PP', what would that tell you?"		 Problem-posing from inverse context. Students apply Lesson 2 properties in reverse. Do students recall that the mirror line passes through the midpoint of PP'?
Observe Phase Worked investigation:
1. Find midpoint M of PP' (M = midpoint formula)
2. Find gradient of PP' (m₁)
3. Mirror line gradient = −1/m₁ (perpendicular)
4. Use point-slope form with M to write the equation
5. Verify: reflect P over the equation found — do you get P'?
Repeat with a second pair of points to confirm.		 MATERIAL: Squared paper, rulers, scientific calculators
RECALL: Midpoint formula, gradient formula, perpendicular gradients (m₁ × m₂ = −1)		 "Step 3 is the key — you learned perpendicular gradients in Strand 1. Use that now."
"Always verify with a second point pair. One match could be coincidence."		 Multi-step problem with explicit connection to prior algebra knowledge (gradients, midpoint, perpendicular lines). Check step 3: are students correctly applying m₁ × m₂ = −1? Common error: using m₁ not −1/m₁ for the mirror line.
Explain Phase Students present solutions to two challenge problems:
• Problem A: Object A(−2,1), B(0,4), C(3,2) and image A'(4,1), B'(2,4), C'(−1,2) — find the mirror line
• Problem B: Object and image given on a Cartesian grid — identify mirror line from the graph
Class discussion: Is the mirror line y = x? y = 1? Some other line?		 MATERIAL: Coordinate geometry exercise sheets
DIGITAL: GeoGebra for checking		 "Explain your method step by step — not just your answer."
"Problem B has a trick: the mirror line does not pass through the origin."		 Worked problems in authentic context. Students apply multi-step algebraic reasoning. Problem A: correct answer is x = 1. Problem B: equation depends on grid. Students who get incorrect equations have errors in gradient or midpoint step.
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Almost all DQB questions should now be answerable. Class works through DQB systematically. Remaining questions (if any) are posted for Lesson 6 to answer. MATERIAL: DQB board, green sticky notes for "ANSWERED" "Which questions from Lesson 1 can FINALLY be answered now?" DQB completion creates a sense of intellectual closure and motivates the final lesson. Any remaining unanswered questions reveal gaps for Lesson 6 to address.
Model Building Phase Major model revision: add the inverse procedure (finding the mirror line) to the model. Model should now show both directions: Object + Mirror → Image AND Object + Image → Mirror. MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 5 revision) "Your model is now complete in BOTH directions. That is real mathematical understanding." Bidirectional model represents full conceptual mastery. Do models clearly show both directions of the reflection relationship?
D. TEACHER REFLECTION
After teaching this lesson, reflect on:

1. Did students recall perpendicular gradients from prior learning? How much revision was needed?

2. The perpendicular bisector approach vs. the gradient/midpoint approach — which did students find more intuitive?

3. Problem A has mirror line x = 1 (vertical). Did students recognise this requires a different approach (no gradient)?

4. Did students verify their equations by testing a point? Or did they stop after finding the equation?

5. DQB: were all questions answered by the end of Lesson 5? What remained for Lesson 6?

LESSON 6: Congruence Tests and Real-World Design Challenge
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents learn the four triangle congruence tests, connect congruence to reflection, and complete a real-world design challenge applying all unit learning.
Knowledge• State and distinguish the four congruence tests: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS
• Explain that a reflected image is always congruent to its object
• Identify which congruence test applies to a given pair of triangles

• Design a symmetric pattern tile using reflection (Kanga/architectural design challenge)
• Write a final explanation connecting reflection, congruence, and the phenomenon
Attitudes• Pride in creating an original design using mathematical principles
• Confidence to explain mathematical ideas clearly in writing
Purpose in StorylineCongruence is the formal connection between reflection and equality. The design challenge applies all five lessons. The final explanation closes the unit narrative.
Safety NotesNo specific safety notes.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Lesson opens by connecting reflection to congruence: "We know reflection preserves size and shape — that means object and image are CONGRUENT. Today we learn the four tests that PROVE congruence formally."

Students work through the four tests (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS) with triangle card-matching activities. Then the design challenge: create a symmetric tile that could be used on a Kenyan wall or kanga textile. Students reflect a base unit over two axes to create a 2×2 tile and label all congruent parts. The lesson closes with each student writing their Final Explanation and comparing Lesson 1 and Lesson 6 models.

Predict Phase Shown two triangles: "Are these congruent? How do you know?" Students write a justification before any instruction.
