LESSON 1: What Is Rotation? Centre, Angle, and Direction
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents observe and describe rotation in Kenyan real-world contexts, identifying the three defining elements: centre of rotation, angle of rotation, and direction.
Knowledge• Define rotation as a transformation that turns a shape around a fixed point
• Identify the centre of rotation, angle of rotation, and direction (clockwise/anticlockwise)
• Distinguish rotation from reflection and translation
Skills• Identify the three rotation parameters in physical and photographic examples
• Estimate angles of rotation from visual inspection
• Use tracing paper to perform simple rotations
Attitudes• Curiosity about the mathematics behind everyday rotating machines
• Appreciation of Kenya's renewable energy investment (Ngong Hills Wind Farm)
Purpose in StorylineThis lesson anchors the driving question in the wind turbine phenomenon. Students build intuitive understanding of the three rotation parameters before formal rules are introduced.
Safety NotesScissors for cutting tracing paper — use safety scissors.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
The lesson opens with a 90-second time-lapse video of the Ngong Hills Wind Farm at night (blades illuminated) and a close-up of a clock second hand. Students discuss: "What is the same about these two motions? What is different?" The class identifies: both have a fixed centre, both move through angles, both have a direction of spin.

Students then use tracing paper over printed diagrams to physically rotate shapes by given angles. The DQB is opened with questions about rotation rules, coordinate effects, and applications. Each student draws their initial model: "What is rotation and how does it work?"

LESSON 2: Rotation About the Origin — Coordinate Rules
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents derive and apply coordinate rules for rotation about the origin through 90°, 180°, and 270°.
Knowledge• State coordinate rules: 90° ACW → (x,y) → (−y,x); 180° → (−x,−y); 270° ACW → (y,−x)
• Explain why 90° CW gives the same image as 270° ACW
• State that 360° rotation maps every point to itself
Skills• Apply coordinate rules to rotate points and polygons about the origin
• Plot images on the Cartesian plane and verify using tracing paper
• Identify the rotation from object and image coordinates
Attitudes• Appreciation that algebraic rules make geometric transformations precise and fast
• Confidence with coordinate manipulation
Purpose in StorylineThe coordinate rules are the algebraic heart of rotation. Students move from the physical tracing-paper model to a precise mathematical rule that can be applied to any point without drawing.
Safety NotesNo specific safety notes.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Students plot triangle A(1,2), B(3,1), C(2,4) on squared paper and rotate it 90° ACW about the origin using tracing paper. They record the image coordinates and look for the pattern. The rule (x,y)→(−y,x) is derived from the data. They then derive rules for 180° and 270° ACW by the same method.

GeoGebra is used to explore fractional and larger angles (e.g., 45°, 135°, 450°) as an extension. The turbine context is revisited: if a blade tip is at coordinates (0,5) — where is it after one-third turn (120°)?

LESSON 3: Rotation About Any Point
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents extend rotation to any centre point — not just the origin — using translation + origin rotation + translation back.
Knowledge• Explain the three-step method: translate so centre becomes origin, rotate, translate back
• Apply the method to rotate a shape about any point
• Verify that the distance from each image vertex to the centre equals the distance from the object vertex
Skills• Rotate a triangle about a centre point that is not the origin
• Apply the three-step coordinate method accurately
• Use tracing paper to verify results
Attitudes• Persistence through multi-step calculations
• Appreciation that complex problems can be broken into simpler steps
Purpose in StorylineGeneralising from the origin to any centre is a key conceptual step. Students see that the origin rule is a special case of a general method.
Safety NotesNo specific safety notes.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Teacher introduces the challenge: "Rotate triangle A(4,5), B(6,3), C(5,7) by 90° ACW about centre P(2,1)." Students attempt it directly — most will struggle or make errors. The teacher then reveals the three-step method: (1) translate so P becomes the origin (subtract P from all coordinates), (2) apply the 90° ACW origin rule, (3) translate back (add P to all coordinates).

Students practise with two further examples and verify with tracing paper. Context: the centre of a gear wheel is at (3,2) — rotate a tooth tip through 60°.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase "Rotate point Q(5,3) by 90° ACW about centre P(2,1). Predict Q'." Students attempt using the origin rule directly (common error: forgetting to adjust for centre). Record predictions — these will likely be wrong and that is intentional. MATERIAL: Squared paper "Try it with the rule (x,y)→(−y,x) directly. Does your answer feel right? Check with tracing paper." Structured failure: applying the origin rule directly to a non-origin centre produces an incorrect answer. This motivates the three-step method. Verify: most predictions will be wrong. Use this as evidence that a new method is needed.
Observe Phase Teacher demonstrates the three-step method with Q(5,3) about P(2,1), 90° ACW:
Step 1: Translate — (5−2, 3−1) = (3,2) [P is now origin]
Step 2: Rotate 90° ACW about origin — (3,2) → (−2,3)
Step 3: Translate back — (−2+2, 3+1) = (0,4) = Q'
Verify with tracing paper: pin on P, rotate 90° ACW — does Q land on (0,4)?
Students apply the same method to point R(6,5) about P(2,1), 90° ACW.		 MATERIAL: Squared paper, tracing paper, rulers
WORKED EXAMPLE: Written on board with all three steps labelled		 "The key insight: if we move P to the origin, we CAN use the Lesson 2 rules. Then we move back."
"What operation moves a point? Translation. What is the translation vector from P to origin?"		 Decomposition strategy: complex problem reduced to three familiar simpler operations. Check Step 1: are students subtracting the centre coordinates? Check Step 3: are they adding the centre coordinates back?
Explain Phase Apply the three-step method to rotate a full triangle:
• Problem A: Rotate triangle A(4,5), B(6,3), C(5,7) by 90° ACW about (2,1)
• Problem B: Rotate quadrilateral PQRS by 180° about centre (3,0)
Partners check each other's work. Class discusses: what is the same in every problem? (The three steps never change — only the centre and angle change.)		 MATERIAL: Coordinate geometry worksheet (2 problems) "Generalise: write the three-step method as a formula using centre point (a,b):
Step 1: (x−a, y−b) → Step 2: apply rule → Step 3: add (a,b) back."		 Students move from specific examples to a generalised formula — algebraic abstraction. Problem A answer: A'(−2,3), B'(0,5), C'(−4,4) — check all three vertices.
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Update DQB. Move "rotation about any point" questions to ANSWERED. New questions: "How do I find the centre if I only have the before and after?" "Are the distances from centre preserved?" MATERIAL: DQB board "The distance question is about PROPERTIES — that is Lesson 4." DQB continues to build the unit narrative. New questions about properties signal readiness for Lesson 4.

Show that the Lesson 2 origin rules are a special case (when centre = origin, Steps 1 and 3 cancel out). MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 3 revision) "Your model should now handle ANY centre. That is a generalisation — one of the most powerful ideas in mathematics." Generalisation as the core mathematical move — specific (origin) becomes general (any point). Do models correctly identify that origin rotation is a special case?

LESSON 4: Properties of Rotation
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents investigate and prove the key properties of rotation: distances and angles are preserved, the centre is equidistant from object and image vertices.
Knowledge• State that rotation preserves lengths (object and image are congruent)
• State that rotation preserves angles (angle between any two lines is preserved)
• State that the centre of rotation is equidistant from any object vertex and its corresponding image vertex
• Distinguish rotation from dilation (rotation preserves size; dilation does not)
Skills• Measure distances from centre to object and image vertices
• Verify angle preservation using a protractor
• Use properties to identify whether a transformation is a rotation
Attitudes• Mathematical rigour: proving properties rather than assuming them
• Appreciation that preserving distances and angles is what makes rotation useful in engineering
Purpose in StorylineProperties confirm that rotation is a rigid transformation (isometry). This connects rotation to congruence (Lesson 2.2) and prepares for the centre-finding method (Lesson 5).
Safety NotesCompass points are sharp — use with care.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
Students begin with a congruence question: "A turbine blade rotates 120°. Is the blade's shape and size preserved, or does rotation distort it?" The answer is yes — preserved. This motivates a formal investigation.

Groups measure distances from centre of rotation to object vertices (OA, OB, OC) and to image vertices (OA', OB', OC'). They compare. They also measure object side lengths and image side lengths. The class derives: rotation is an isometry (rigid transformation). Finally, students learn to use the equidistance property to IDENTIFY the centre — by drawing circles around corresponding vertex pairs.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase "After rotating triangle ABC 90° ACW about origin O, is triangle A'B'C' congruent to ABC? Which of the four congruence tests (from Lesson 2.2) would prove it? Predict before measuring." MATERIAL: Previously drawn rotation diagrams from Lesson 2 or 3 "Predict: are the side lengths the same? The angles? What do you expect from a 'turning' transformation?" Connecting rotation to congruence (prior unit) creates cross-topic coherence. Do students predict congruence correctly? Do they suggest SSS as the test?
Observe Phase Measurement investigation:
1. On a rotation diagram (triangle rotated about O), measure: OA, OA', OB, OB', OC, OC'.
2. Compare and tabulate.
3. Measure side lengths: AB, A'B', BC, B'C'.
4. Measure angles: ∠ABC and ∠A'B'C'.
5. Conclusion: what is preserved?		 MATERIAL: Protractors, rulers, rotation diagrams (printed, accurately constructed)
DIGITAL: GeoGebra to dynamically show that properties hold for ANY angle		 "Measure to the nearest millimetre. Do OA and OA' match exactly?"
"What word describes a transformation that preserves all lengths and angles?"		 Empirical verification of properties. Measurement confirms what coordinate algebra implies. Results: OA = OA', OB = OB', OC = OC'; side lengths preserved; angles preserved. Deviations larger than 0.5 mm indicate construction errors.
Explain Phase Formally state three properties:
1. Distances preserved: |OP| = |OP'| for every point P
2. Lengths preserved: |AB| = |A'B'| (rotation is an isometry)
3. Angles preserved: ∠ABC = ∠A'B'C'
Isometry: a transformation that preserves all distances. (Rotation, reflection, and translation are all isometries.)
Application: use property 1 to identify the centre — the centre is the point equidistant from P and P' for ALL corresponding pairs.		 MATERIAL: Whiteboard; student measurement tables "The centre is the UNIQUE point equidistant from each object-image pair. How would you find it geometrically? (Hint: circles or perpendicular bisectors.)" Formal statement of properties from empirical findings. Centre-finding hint previews Lesson 5. "True or false: rotation changes the size of a shape." (False — rotation is an isometry.) "True or false: rotation changes the orientation of a shape." (True — orientation is reversed for reflection, but for rotation it is preserved; clarify this common confusion.)
Driving Question Board (DQB) Creation Update DQB. Move "properties" questions to ANSWERED. Focus on remaining question: "How do I find the centre and angle if I only have object and image?" MATERIAL: DQB board "That is exactly what Lesson 5 teaches. The equidistance property is the key." DQB creates direct anticipation for Lesson 5. Students should now be comfortable with isometry and ready for centre-finding.
Model Building Phase Update Rotation Model: add the three properties. Label the model diagram with: distance from centre to object vertex = distance from centre to image vertex. MATERIAL: Model Journal page (Lesson 4 revision) "Your model should now prove that rotation preserves congruence — using SSS." Model integrates isometry concept with congruence knowledge from prior unit. Do models correctly state all three properties? Do they connect to congruence tests?
D. TEACHER REFLECTION
After teaching this lesson, reflect on:

1. Did students connect rotation properties to congruence (Sub-Strand 2.2) effectively? Which congruence test did they apply?

What did I observe?Measured distances from centre of rotation to object vertices (OA, OB, OC) and image vertices (OA', OB', OC'): all equal. Measured side lengths and angles: all preserved. Verified using GeoGebra for dynamic confirmation.
What did I learn?Three properties of rotation: (1) distances from centre preserved (OA = OA'), (2) side lengths preserved (isometry), (3) angles preserved. Rotation is an isometry — it does NOT change the size or shape of the figure.
How does this explain the phenomenon?A wind turbine blade must preserve its shape and size as it rotates — otherwise the blade would distort and fail. These three properties mathematically guarantee that every point on the blade stays exactly the same distance from the hub, sweeping a perfect circle. This is why rotation is used in engineering: it is predictable and precise.
LESSON 5: Finding the Centre and Angle of Rotation
A. SPECIFIC LEARNING OUTCOMES
PurposeStudents determine the centre and angle of rotation given an object and its image, completing the full inverse problem of rotation.
Knowledge• State that the centre of rotation lies on the perpendicular bisector of any segment PP'
• Use two perpendicular bisectors to find the centre of rotation by intersection
• Calculate the angle of rotation using the angle ∠POP'
Skills• Construct perpendicular bisectors of at least two PP' segments
• Find their intersection to locate the centre
• Use a protractor to measure ∠POP' to find the angle of rotation
Attitudes• Persistence with multi-step constructions
• Appreciation that inverse problems are as important as direct problems
Purpose in StorylineThe inverse problem — finding centre and angle — completes the rotation model. Students can now move in both directions: apply a rotation OR identify one from evidence.
Safety NotesCompass use — sharp points, handle carefully.
B. LESSON OVERVIEW
The lesson opens with an engineering challenge: "A gear tooth has moved from position A to position A'. Find the centre of the gear wheel (the centre of rotation) and the angle it has turned."

Students recall the Lesson 4 property: the centre is equidistant from P and P'. This means it lies on the perpendicular bisector of PP'. Construct two perpendicular bisectors — their intersection IS the centre. Then measure ∠POP' for the angle. Students apply the method to two challenge problems.
C. LESSON IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK
Phase Learner Experience Resource Teacher Actions Sensemaking Strategy Assessment Strategy
Predict Phase "You are given triangle ABC and its image A'B'C' but NOT the centre or angle. From what you know about rotation properties — how would you find the centre? Predict the METHOD before we derive it." MATERIAL: Printed diagrams showing object and image triangles "Think about Lesson 4: the centre is equidistant from P and P'. What geometric construction finds a point equidistant from two given points?" Predicting the method activates Lesson 4 knowledge and creates ownership of the derivation. Do students recall perpendicular bisector from Lesson 2.2? If not, brief revision needed.
Observe Phase Step-by-step construction:
1. Identify one corresponding pair: A and A'.
2. Construct the perpendicular bisector of AA'. (Centre lies somewhere on this line.)
3. Construct the perpendicular bisector of BB'.
4. The intersection of the two perpendicular bisectors is the centre O.
5. Draw OA. Draw OA'. Measure ∠AOA' — this is the angle of rotation.
6. Verify: construct perpendicular bisector of CC'. Does it also pass through O?		 MATERIAL: Ruler, compass, protractor, printed diagrams
